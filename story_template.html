The TikTok Lottery

A creator with 28 million followers posts a video. So does an account almost nobody follows. Thirty days later, the nobody has eight million views. This is not a fluke — it's how the platform works.

By Yehor Larcenko, Olha, Ivanna and Davyd
Published June 12, 2026 · Data: June–November 2024

On most platforms, audience is destiny. A newspaper with a million subscribers reaches a million readers; a TV channel's ratings move slowly, year by year. TikTok made a different bet: every video, from anyone, starts almost from zero, and the algorithm decides who sees it next.

To see what that bet means in practice, we followed 209,543 videos posted by 1,872 creators between June and November 2024 — every video tracked day by day for a month after posting, alongside each creator's follower count.

Let's start with the biggest account in the data.

Part 2 · 30 days in the machine

The first week decides almost everything — except when it doesn't

Follower counts are a creator's story. Each video has its own. We took every video with a full 30-day history and traced how its views accumulated, day by day, from the moment of posting.

Every line is one video's first month, drawn from a sample of 1,183 (note the log scale).

Most of the action happens in days 0–7; after that, the curves go flat.

THE OVERNIGHT HIT

On November 4th, an account with 14,000 followers posted a cooking video. Four million views in the first week. 8.4 million by day 30.

That's 600 views for every single follower.

THE SLOW BURNER

This video was dead on arrival: 1,245 views after an entire week.

Then the algorithm changed its mind. By day 30: 12.9 million. Nobody — including its creator — can plan this.

THE FLOP

But this is the line to remember, because it's the typical one. Half of all videos end their month below 641 views.

The hits you just saw are the lottery's advertising. This is the ticket most people hold.

Part 3 · The payout table

One percent of videos takes two-thirds of everything

Lotteries are defined by their payout tables. Here is TikTok's, computed across all 209,543 videos.

The distribution of final view counts. The median video gets 641 views; the average is 31,600 — fifty times higher, dragged up by a handful of monsters on the right.

Whenever a mean is 50× the median, you're looking at a lottery.

The pink tail is the top 1% of videos.

Those 2,095 videos collected 64% of all views in the dataset. The remaining 207,448 videos shared the rest.

So do followers matter or not? Both. Each grid is 100 typical videos, grouped by the creator's audience size.

Under 10K followers, 88 of 100 videos die below 1,000 views. Above 1M, the typical video still reaches only ~91,000 — and 1 in 100 still flops completely.

Followers buy you more tickets. They don't buy the prize.

Part 4 · Loading the dice

What actually moves the odds

If virality is a lottery, content choices are how you load the dice. We compared median outcomes across what a creator controls: topic, length, emotional tone, and whether the video was AI-generated. The differences are real — and smaller than anyone selling a "viral formula" admits.

Dominant emotion

The machine that promised to free creators from the tyranny of follower counts replaced it with something stranger: a system where a cooking video from nowhere outdraws a 28-million-follower account, where a dead video resurrects itself in week two, and where the only reliable strategy is the lottery player's strategy — buy more tickets.

Half of everything posted disappears under a thousand views. One percent takes two-thirds of the prize pool. And six hundred thousand new tickets get printed every day.

Methods & data

Data: lingbow/tiktok-video-engagement-200k (CC BY-NC 4.0) — 209,543 TikTok videos by 1,872 creators, posted 24 June – 9 November 2024, with daily engagement and follower panels. Views = cumulative play count on the last observed day. Lifelines use the 73,940 videos tracked for ≥30 days. Creator follower counts are averaged over the observation window; creator-level charts include creators with ≥5 videos. Medians are used throughout because of the extreme skew. Topic and emotion labels are model-derived by the dataset authors; engagement numbers are platform data.
